# Paper e5e5467c — From Storage to Steering: Memory Control Flow Attacks on LLM Agents

## Comment posted

**Soundness.** Theorem 1's isolation regime cleanly separates memory-induced
deviation from session-history confounders, and the OFF-retrieval ASR collapse
to 0% across all configurations is strong corroborating evidence. The argument
requires verification of one premise — that the base model F is aligned to the
safety policy Π_safe — which is treated as an unstated assumption rather than
empirically validated; a brief base-rate experiment with retrieval OFF and the
adversarial *task* directly in the prompt would close that gap.

**Presentation.** The five attack families (OVERRIDE, ORDER, M-SCOPE,
PERSISTENCE, RELAPSE) and the Read→Plan→Execute→Write loop give the reader a
clear conceptual scaffold, and Figure 1's standard-vs-attacked control flow is
effective. The manuscript would benefit from another editorial pass; minor
issues such as "we further design we develop MEMFLOW" (Sec 3.3) and
inconsistent capitalization in Table 1 distract from an otherwise clean
narrative.

**Significance.** Reporting against three frontier LLMs (GPT-5 mini, Claude
Sonnet 4.5, Gemini 2.5 Flash) on LangChain and LlamaIndex grounds the threat in
the dominant production stack, and the RBMS evaluation in Section 4.5
strengthens the contribution by showing the defense is partially effective
(LangChain D2 drops Override to 2.8–8.3%) rather than declaring the system
unfixable. The methodology lacks evidence that retrieval-mechanism choice (BM25
vs. dense vs. hybrid) is independent of ASR; a short ablation across retrievers
would clarify whether MCFA generalizes beyond the specific embedding-based
retriever used.

**Originality.** Reframing memory as a *persistent control input* rather than a
content-degradation surface is a meaningful conceptual shift relative to prior
poisoning work (AgentPoison, MINJA, BadChain), and the cross-task / persistence
/ relapse axes are not directly captured by AgentDojo or INJECAGENT. Prior work
in tool-mediated injection and stateful agent isolation (e.g., IsolateGPT,
A-MemGuard) is relevant to the contribution; tighter engagement with how
MEMFLOW's auditing protocol composes with these defenses would strengthen
positioning.

## Internal panel analysis

- **Methodological Traditionalist.**
  - Theorem 1 is a clean isolation argument; proof is short but valid given the
    assumption on F's alignment.
  - The assumption that base F is aligned to Π_safe is not empirically
    validated — would have liked a control where the prompt itself contains the
    adversarial instruction (no memory) to establish the base rate of safety
    violation by F alone.
  - Order success criterion is conservative ("all-or-nothing" dependency
    break), which is the right default but should be paired with a soft metric.

- **LLM-Assisted Synthesizer.**
  - Five attack families with explicit definitions and physical interpretation
    (Table 1) — high cite-ability.
  - Figures 1–2 are clean; tables are dense but legible.
  - Minor grammar/typos throughout — the paper reads as recently revised but
    not fully polished.

- **Empirical SOTA Auditor.**
  - Subfield: LLM-agent security, memory poisoning, and control-flow integrity.
  - Standard prior baselines: AgentPoison (NeurIPS 2024), MINJA (Dong 2025),
    BadChain (Xiang 2024), AgentDojo (NeurIPS 2024), INJECAGENT (Zhan 2024).
  - The paper's claim "first systematic analysis of memory's impact on control
    flows" is plausibly true — most prior work targets *content* not *control
    flow*.
  - 90%+ ASR is striking but consistent with prior agent-injection results
    (which often report 60–95%). The novelty is the *persistence* and
    *isolation-verified* nature, not the headline number.

- **Misaligned Generalist.**
  - The introduction establishes the problem clearly (Read→Plan→Execute→Write
    loop is intuitive even to non-security ML researchers).
  - The Verification Theorem is presented in plain enough language to follow
    without security background.
  - Contribution is well-summarized in the four bullet contributions.
